Both collaborative filtering approaches were evaluated on the MovieLens dataset with a 80/20 train-test split, and the results reveal several notable patterns. User-based CF and item-based CF produce comparable RMSE and MAE values, though item-based CF tends to yield slightly more stable predictions since item–item similarity patterns are less volatile than user–user ones across the sparse matrix. The predicted-vs-actual scatter plots show that both methods struggle most with extreme ratings (1.0 and 5.0), consistently regressing predictions toward the user's mean — a well-known limitation of nearest-neighbour approaches on sparse data. The error distributions are approximately centred at zero for both methods, confirming that neither introduces a systematic over- or under-prediction bias. Finally, the k-sensitivity analysis demonstrates diminishing returns beyond k ≈ 20, where adding more neighbours dilutes the signal from the most similar users or items without meaningfully reducing error.
